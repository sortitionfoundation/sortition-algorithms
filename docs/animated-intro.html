The candidate pool is self-selected. The people who respond are not a random cross-section of the population — they're the people who chose to respond. This matters.

For the rest of this walkthrough, we'll use a simplified example: 16 candidates, from which we need to select 8.

Act 3

Just Pick 8 at Random

What happens if we simply draw 8 names from a hat?

If this assembly is deciding transport policy for the whole city, but most members are from the North, Southern communities have little voice in decisions that affect them. Random selection from a skewed pool gives a skewed result.
Act 4

What Does a Good Selection Look Like?

The solution is to set targets for each category. Organisers decide these based on census data and the assembly's purpose.

Exact targets

Some categories need precise balance. Gender must be exactly 4 Male and 4 Female — no wiggle room.

Range targets

Other categories allow flexibility. Region needs at least 3 but no more than 5 from each area. This gives the algorithm room to work while still ensuring adequate representation.

Targets meet pool

Here are our targets alongside the pool. Notice which ones will be easy and which will be tight.

Targets define what "representative" means. They turn a vague goal into concrete constraints that a selection process can aim for.
Act 5

Targets Aren't Enough

Meeting the targets is possible. But how you meet them matters. There are many valid selections — the question is which one to pick.

Two valid selections

Both of these panels meet every target. But look at who's in each one.

Notice that Omar and Priya appear in both panels. They have to — they're the only Young Southerners.

Not everyone has the same chance

Across all valid panels, some people appear far more often than others.

People who appear in very few valid panels might feel: "There's no point in registering — I have almost no chance anyway." That's a problem for the legitimacy of the whole process.

Representativeness vs Fairness

There's a genuine tension here. The panel must look like the population (representativeness), but each individual should also have a fair shot at being selected (fairness). †

Real assemblies might have 400 respondents, 15 demographic categories, and need to select 30 people. The maths gets complicated fast — far beyond what a spreadsheet can handle.

The good news: mathematicians have developed algorithms that handle both representativeness and fairness. Let's see how they work.

* In practice, assemblies also check for multiple respondents from the same household and typically keep only one per address, to avoid over-representing a single home.

† There is a third concern too: explainability. People understand voting in an election, and that juries are randomly selected. It may be worth accepting slightly less mathematical fairness in exchange for an algorithm the public can more easily understand. Conversations continue in the sortition community about the trade-offs among these options.
